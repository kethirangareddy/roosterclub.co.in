Gamefowl around the world: famous heritage breeds by country

Rooster Club knowledge series · ~9 min read

Long before borders were drawn on maps, people across the world were keeping tall, proud, hard-feathered birds for their beauty, their bearing and their place in local culture. Today these heritage "gamefowl" breeds are kept and shown for their looks, their history and their value as breeding and exhibition stock. This tour travels country by country through the most famous of them — useful background for any breeder building a collection or a buyer trying to recognise what they are looking at.

India — the Aseel (Asil)

India's own Aseel is among the oldest and most influential heritage breeds in the world. Tall, broad-chested, with a small pea comb, close hard feathering and an upright carriage, the Aseel comes in named colour types such as Dega, Kaki, Nemali, Reza and Peela. Its bloodlines have been carried into countless breeds elsewhere, which is why you will hear "Asil blood" mentioned from Pakistan to the Americas. For Indian keepers it remains the gold standard of conformation and presence.

Thailand — the Gai Chon and Thai gamefowl

Thailand's native birds are lean, leggy and famously intelligent, with a graceful upright stance. Thai gamefowl are bred with enormous care for colour, structure and temperament, and the country has a deep show culture around them. They have also been exported widely as ornamental and heritage stock.

Japan — the Shamo

The Shamo (a name derived from "Siam", reflecting its Thai-influenced ancestry) is a towering, muscular bird recognised internationally as a heritage exhibition breed. Japan refined it over centuries into several sizes, from the giant O-Shamo to the bantam Nankin-Shamo, and today it is a prized show bird in poultry standards around the world.

The Philippines

Filipino breeders are renowned for meticulous record-keeping and selective breeding, and the country has one of the most active gamefowl cultures anywhere. Many internationally known bloodlines have been developed and refined there, and Filipino lines are sought after by collectors and breeders globally.

United States — American gamefowl

American breeders spent the nineteenth and twentieth centuries blending Old English, Irish, Asil and other stock into distinctive named bloodlines kept for their plumage, conformation and history. These lines are documented in detailed pedigrees and traded among heritage-poultry enthusiasts and exhibition breeders.

England — the Old English Game

The Old English Game is one of the cornerstones of the modern poultry fancy. Standardised by British poultry clubs, it survives today as a celebrated exhibition breed shown in a dazzling range of colours, and it sits behind many ornamental bantams kept purely for their looks.

Spain and Latin America

Spanish heritage fowl travelled with traders and settlers across the Atlantic, seeding strong breeding cultures in Mexico, Cuba, Peru and beyond. Each region developed its own preferences in colour and structure, and Spanish-descended lines remain a distinct and respected branch of the worldwide gamefowl family.

What unites every country on this list is the same instinct: to select, over generations, for a bird that is healthy, striking and unmistakably its own.

Why this matters to an Indian breeder

Knowing the world map of heritage breeds helps you in two practical ways. First, it lets you recognise crosses and influences in birds you are offered — "Asil blood", "Shamo type", "OEG colour" all mean something specific. Second, it helps you describe and value your own stock accurately to buyers who know these references. When you list a bird on Rooster Club, naming its type and lineage clearly — with honest photos and a verified-seller profile — is exactly what lets a knowledgeable buyer pay what genuine heritage stock is worth.

Keeping the heritage alive, responsibly

These breeds have survived because generations of keepers valued them as living heritage — for show, conservation and the simple pride of a beautiful bird. Kept that way, lawfully and with good welfare, they connect a backyard in Telangana to a centuries-old global tradition. That is the spirit Rooster Club exists to support: helping keepers buy, sell and celebrate fine birds with people who appreciate them.
