Aseel breed types & colours: Dega, Kaki, Nemali, Reza, Savala explained

Rooster Club knowledge series · ~9 min read

Aseel is not one bird but a family. Across South India, breeders name birds by their colour pattern, their build, and sometimes their region — and a single rooster might be described by two or three of these at once. If you are buying or breeding Aseel, learning to read these names is what separates an informed buyer from someone paying a high price for the wrong bird. This guide walks through the main colour types and build types in plain language, so you can describe what you have, ask the right questions, and recognise genuine stock.

1. Colour names every Aseel keeper should know

Most traditional Aseel names describe the plumage colour of the cock. The exact word changes by region and language, but these are the ones you will hear most across Telugu- and Tamil-speaking areas:

  • Dega (Setha / white) — a light or white-bodied bird. Often prized and one of the most recognised lines; "Dega" is widely used for the larger, lighter-coloured classic Aseel.
  • Kaki — a crow-black bird, dark plumage through the body. Named for the crow (kaki) it resembles.
  • Nemali — peacock-coloured, with a green-and-blue iridescent sheen across dark feathers, named after the peacock (nemali).
  • Reza (Rampuri type) — a mixed or speckled red-and-light pattern; "Reza" broadly points to a part-coloured, lighter, often quicker bird rather than one solid shade.
  • Savala — a grey or ash-coloured pattern, often with a salt-and-pepper or barred look through the hackle and back.
  • Peela — golden-red, the classic warm red-and-gold plumage; one of the most common and sought-after colours.
  • Yarkin / Java — black with red or golden flecking; a darker bird with warm highlights.
  • Mushka / Teekar / Chitta — other regional names you may meet for black, mottled and white patterns respectively.

Remember: these are colour descriptions, not separate species. The same bloodline can throw birds of more than one colour, and sellers in different districts use slightly different words for the same shade.

A colour name tells you what a bird looks like — not automatically how it was bred. Always ask about the line and the parents, not just the colour.

2. Build and size types

Alongside colour, Aseel are described by body type, and this matters as much as plumage:

  • Rajah / Big Aseel — tall, heavy, broad-chested birds with thick bone and an upright stance. Slower and more powerful in build.
  • Reza (light type) — a smaller, lighter, more agile bird; the word doubles as both a colour and a build description in many areas.
  • Kulang / Madras Aseel — the very tall, long-legged Tamil Nadu type, known for height and reach.
  • Mianwali / Sindhi and other imported lines — heavier Pakistani-origin Aseel that some breeders cross in for size and bone.

A serious buyer always reads colour and build together — for example a "Peela Rajah" is a golden-red bird of the large heavy type, while a "Reza Peela" is a lighter, quicker golden bird.

3. How to read a genuine Aseel

Whatever the colour, true Aseel share a recognisable stamp. Look for a pea comb (small, tight, three-ridged — not a large single comb), a short curved beak, a broad muscular body, close hard feathering, strong thick legs, and a bold upright carriage. The face is usually clean and the wattles small. A bird with a big floppy single comb, loose soft feathering or a narrow frame is very likely crossed with non-Aseel stock. Colour can be faked or coincidental; bone, comb and carriage are much harder to fake.

4. Why the type matters when you buy

Naming matters because price follows reputation. A buyer paying for a "Dega" or a pure "Kaki" line is paying for a known look and a known ancestry — and that is exactly where misdescription happens. The protection is simple: ask to see the parent birds, ask about the line by name, and check the seller's track record. On Rooster Club you can view a seller's other listings, ratings and past reviews before you commit, and message them directly with clear photos — so you can match the colour and build to what is actually described before any money changes hands.

5. Recording your own birds

If you breed, write down each bird's colour name, build type, parentage and any distinctive markings. Over a couple of seasons this record becomes your most valuable asset: it lets you describe stock accurately to buyers, choose pairings deliberately, and command a premium because you can prove what a bird is. Learn the names, then trust your eye

Start by learning the handful of colours and builds above, then spend time looking at real birds beside their parents. The names give you a shared language with other breeders; the comb, bone and carriage tell you whether the bird is genuine. Together they let you buy, sell and breed Aseel with confidence instead of guesswork.
