Natu Kodi / country chicken farming: a beginner's guide

Rooster Club knowledge series · ~9 min read

Country chicken — Natu Kodi in Telugu, desi murgi in Hindi, nati koli in Kannada — sells for two to three times the price of broiler meat because buyers prize its firm texture, deep flavour and natural rearing. For a small farmer, a country chicken unit needs modest land and capital, and demand almost always outstrips supply. This guide covers the essentials to start well.

1. Why country chicken pays

Broiler farming is a high-volume, low-margin business dominated by large integrators. Country chicken is the opposite: lower volume, higher margin, and a market you can serve directly. Birds are hardier, tolerate local conditions, forage for part of their feed, and command a premium at the farm gate. The trade-off is slower growth — a country bird reaches market weight in four to six months rather than six weeks — so your edge is price and quality, not speed.

2. Choose the right breed

Match the breed to your goal:

  • Aseel — premium gamefowl and table bird; slow-growing but highly valued, especially for breeding stock.
  • Kadaknath — the black-meat bird; a niche premium market with strong demand and high prices per bird.
  • Giriraja / Gramapriya / Vanaraja — improved dual-purpose birds bred for backyards; faster growth and better egg numbers than pure desi, while keeping a country look and taste.
  • Local desi — fully adapted to your area, excellent foragers, best disease resistance, but the slowest growers.

Many farmers start with an improved dual-purpose bird for cash flow and keep a small pure line for breeding and premium sales.

3. Housing and system

You have three broad options:

  • Free-range — birds roam by day, return to a simple night shelter. Lowest feed cost, best flavour, needs safe land and predator protection.
  • Semi-intensive — a covered shed plus a fenced run. The most popular balance for serious small farms.
  • Deep litter — fully housed on a dry litter bed (paddy husk or sawdust). Best where land or predators are a problem.

Whatever you choose, give roughly one square foot per growing bird indoors, keep the floor dry, ensure cross-ventilation without direct draughts, and provide clean water and shade at all times. Wet litter is the root of most disease.

4. Feeding without wasting money

Feed is your biggest cost, so don't overspend on commercial feed a country bird doesn't need. A practical approach: feed a balanced chick starter for the first six to eight weeks when the foundation is laid, then shift to a grower ration supplemented heavily by foraging, kitchen and farm scraps, greens, and grains like broken maize and rice bran. Always provide grit and clean water. Birds that forage well can cut your feed bill substantially.

Rule of thumb: spend on quality feed early when it builds the bird, and let foraging do more of the work later.

5. Vaccination and health

Disease, not feed cost, is what wipes out small flocks. The single most important vaccine is for Ranikhet (Newcastle disease), which can kill an entire flock. Follow your local veterinary schedule for Ranikhet, Marek's and Gumboro, deworm regularly, and quarantine every new bird for two to three weeks before mixing it with your flock. Our biosecurity guide covers this in detail.

6. Selling at a good price

The mistake most new farmers make is raising good birds and then selling them cheap to the first middleman who shows up. You earn the premium by reaching buyers directly — neighbours, local hotels and the wider market of people who specifically want country birds. List your birds with clear photos, breed, age and weight, build a few honest reviews, and buyers will come back to you and pay more. Start small, learn fast

Begin with 25–50 birds, get one cycle right, then scale. A small flock teaches you housing, feed and disease management at low risk — and by the time you expand, you'll have the reputation and repeat buyers that make country chicken farming genuinely profitable.
